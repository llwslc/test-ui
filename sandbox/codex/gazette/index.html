DESIGN / SPECIAL REPORT

Typography Declares Its Independence

After years trapped in rounded cards, the written word has returned to the front page — large, urgent and gloriously specific.

BY THE EDITORIAL SYSTEMS DESK · 8 MIN READ

At precisely nine this morning, the grid stopped apologising. Headlines expanded across five columns, rules became visible again, and information remembered that hierarchy can be both useful and beautiful. Witnesses described the change as immediate. Buttons became labels; labels became invitations; the page found a rhythm in the tension between dense report and open margin. The Gazette kit treats typography not as content poured into containers, but as architecture. Hairline rules organise without decoration. Ink blue marks action without shouting. Every hover earns its underline. Every column carries a clear measure, a deliberate rag, and the slight material irregularity of type pressed into newsprint. This is not nostalgia. It is a working editorial machine, built for subjects with consequences and readers with curiosity.

“The page found its voice when the boxes went quiet.”
